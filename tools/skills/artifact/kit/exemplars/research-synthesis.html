Why production agents fail: what 52 sources actually agree on

52 sources·31 incident postmortems· 14 practitioner interviews·synthesized Jul 3, 2026

The public conversation blames model capability; the evidence mostly doesn't. Across postmortems, papers, and interviews, failures concentrate in the scaffolding around the model — context management, tool interfaces, and evaluation practices that measure the wrong unit. One question stays genuinely contested: whether tighter guardrails reduce failures or just relocate them.

Findings

pattern: evidence-card

Most production failures are context-management failures, not model failures.

High confidence
19 of 52 sources · strongest evidence: incident postmortems (11)

Teams that instrumented their agents traced the majority of bad outputs to stale, truncated, or contradictory context — the model behaved reasonably given what it was shown. Capability upgrades didn't fix these; context discipline did.

Evidence (3 of 19)

"We upgraded models twice chasing this bug. The fix was deleting 40k tokens of accumulated tool output that contradicted the current state."

POSTMORTEMCheckout-agent incident review, fintech platform·2025

"Retrieval quality explained 3× more variance in task success than model choice across our 1,200-session sample."

PAPERLong-horizon agent reliability study·2026

"Every on-call page I got last quarter was context rot. Not one was the model being dumb."

INTERVIEWStaff engineer, agent infrastructure team·2026

Eval suites overestimate reliability because they score tasks, not sessions.

Moderate confidence
11 sources · mostly academic; thin on industry replication

Benchmarks measure single, well-posed tasks. Production work is a session: goals drift, state accumulates, and errors compound across turns. Sources report benchmark-to-production gaps of 20–40 points on the same underlying model.

Evidence (2 of 11)

"Per-task pass rate was 91%. Per-session — the unit users experience — it was 54%."

PAPERSession-level evaluation of coding agents·2025

"Our leaderboard position told us nothing about week-two retention. Compounding error did."

BLOGAgent product team retro·2026

Multi-agent systems fail through coordination, and the failure is usually silent.

Moderate confidence
9 sources · all post-2024; the phenomenon is young

Sub-agents don't crash — they quietly diverge: duplicated work, dropped handoffs, one agent undoing another's changes. Detection lags because each agent's local transcript looks fine; only the composed result is wrong.

Evidence (2 of 9)

"Two writer agents each 'fixed' the config, alternating, 14 times. Neither log looked abnormal in isolation."

POSTMORTEMOrchestration deadlock write-up·2026

"Coordination overhead grew faster than fan-out gains beyond four concurrent agents on interdependent tasks."

PAPERMulti-agent scaling measurement·2026

Do tighter guardrails actually reduce failures? The sources split.

Contested
13 sources · 7 supporting, 6 contradicting · split tracks org size

Larger orgs report guardrails catching real incidents; smaller teams report failures migrating to the approval boundary itself — rubber-stamped prompts, workarounds, and automation that routes around the check. The disagreement looks structural, not evidentiary.

Supporting

"Mandatory plan review caught 60% of would-be destructive actions in our first quarter."

POSTMORTEMEnterprise rollout report·2025
Contradicting
